# child-mental-health-jsna-analysis
Using OHID public health data to analyse child mental health inequalities, self-harm admissions, deprivation patterns, and prevention priorities through Power BI and SQL.

## What I Did

I analyzed hospital admission data for self-harm and mental health conditions among children and young people (aged 10-24) across 150 English local authorities.

The project involved:
- Extracting data from OHID Fingertips (self-harm admissions, MH admissions, school absence, deprivation scores)
- Cleaning and merging datasets in Power BI
- Creating inequality analysis by deprivation level
- Developing prevention priority scoring
- Building interactive 3-page dashboard

- ## Key Findings

**Inequality is the defining feature:**
The gap between highest and lowest self-harm admission rates is over 700 per 100,000 - meaning some areas have rates nearly 10x higher than others.

**Deprivation drives outcomes:**
Young people in the most deprived areas consistently show worse mental health outcomes. This isn't random variation - it's systematic inequality.

**Geographic hotspots:**
North Tyneside (785/100k), Northumberland (740/100k), and Swindon (681/100k) show the highest self-harm admission rates.

**Prevention priorities:**
Using a composite score, areas like North Somerset, Herefordshire, and Torbay emerge as highest priority for targeted intervention.

- ## Limitations

- Three local authorities excluded due to missing data (Rutland, Isles of Scilly, City of London)
- Hospital admissions data only captures severe cases reaching emergency care
- Does not include community mental health service data
- Cross-sectional analysis (2023/24 snapshot, not trends over time)
